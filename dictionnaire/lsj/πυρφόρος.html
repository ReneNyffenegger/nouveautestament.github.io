πυρφόρος (parox.), ον, fire-bearing, esp. of lightning, π. κεραυνός Pi.N.10.71, A.Th.444, S.OC1658; ἀστραπαί Id.OT200 (lyr.); Διὸς ἔγχος Ar.Av.1749 (lyr.); πυρφόρος αἰθέρος ἀστήρ Id.Th.1050 (lyr.).     b. π. οἰστοί arrows with combustibles tied to them, so that they may set fire to woodwork, Th.2.75, Arr.An.2.21.3; τοῖς μὲν π . . . τοῖς δ’ ἄλλοις βέλεσι D.S.20.96; οἱ π. ibid.; πυρφόρα, τά, ib.88; πυρφόρος, ὁ, engine for throwing fire, fire-dart, Plb.21.7.1 (dub.), Jul.Or.2.62d.     II. in special senses,     1. epith. of several divinities, as of Zeus in reference to his lightnings, S.Ph.1198 (anap.); of Demeter, prob. in reference to the torches used by her worshippers, E.Supp.260; similarly π. θεαί of Demeter and Persephone, IG4.666.9 (Lerna), E.Ph.687 (lyr.); π. Ἀρτέμιδος αἴγλας S.OT206 (lyr.); Προμηθεὺς π. the Fire-bringer, title of a satyric play of A., cf.S.OC55; also of Capaneus, A.Th.432, S.Ant.135 (lyr.); of Eros, AP5.87 (Rufin.); but, θεὸς π. the fire-bearing god, the god who produces plague or fever, S.OT27.     2. bearer of sacred fire in the worship of Asclepius, Ἀσκληπιοῦ δμῶα π. IG3.693; of the Syrian Goddess, Luc.Syr.D.42.     b. πυρφόρος, ὁ, in the Spartan army, the priest who kept the sacrificial fire, which was never allowed to go out, X.Lac.13.2: hence prov. of a total defeat, ἔδει δὲ μηδὲ πυρφόρον . . περιγενέσθαι Hdt.8.6, cf. D.C.39.45; οὐκ ἔσται π. (v.l. πυροφόρος) τῷ οἴκῳ Ἠσαύ LXXOb.18.     3. π. ἡ ἐκ Δελφῶν bearer of sacred fire from Delphi, SIG711 D 22 (ii B.C.), cf. 728 I (i B.C.); Φοίβου πυρφόροι IG4.666.15 (Lerna); also in a Bacchic thiasos, AJA37.253 (Latium, ii A.D.).
